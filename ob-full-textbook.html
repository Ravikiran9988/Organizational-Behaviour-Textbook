Unit One · Foundations
Organizational Behaviour

1.1 What is Organizational Behaviour (OB)?

Definition

"Organizational Behaviour is the study of human behaviour in organizational settings, the interface between human behaviour and the organization, and the organization itself."

In simple words, OB is the study of how people act at work — individually and in groups — and how that behaviour affects the performance of an organization.

Think of it like this: You join a company. You have your own personality, habits, and emotions. Your colleagues are different. Your manager leads in a certain way. All of this — how you all behave together — is what OB tries to understand and improve.

💡 Real-Life Example

Why does one team work efficiently while another team in the same company always fights? OB answers these kinds of questions.

1.2 Need and Importance of OB

Why do we study Organizational Behaviour? Here are the key reasons:

  • Understanding People: Organizations are made of people. To run them well, you must understand human nature, motivations, and behaviour.
  • Improving Productivity: OB helps identify what motivates employees, which boosts performance and efficiency.
  • Managing Conflicts: Conflicts are common in any workplace. OB gives tools to handle disagreements professionally.
  • Better Leadership: OB helps leaders understand their own style and how to influence others effectively.
  • Reducing Employee Turnover: Happy and motivated employees stay longer. OB helps create a positive work environment.
  • Adapting to Change: In today's fast-changing world, organizations must adapt quickly. OB helps manage that change smoothly.
  • Better Communication: OB teaches how communication flows in organizations and how to improve it.

1.3 Nature of Organizational Behaviour

  • It is a Science: OB uses scientific methods — research, data, and observations — to study behaviour.
  • It is an Art: Applying OB knowledge to manage people requires skill and creativity.
  • It is Interdisciplinary: OB borrows ideas from Psychology, Sociology, Anthropology, and Economics.
  • It is Humanistic: It believes people have real feelings and motivations — not just robotic workers.
  • It is Situational: There is no one-size-fits-all solution. The right approach depends on the situation.
  • It is Goal-Oriented: The ultimate aim is to improve organizational effectiveness.

Scope of OB

OB covers three levels of study:

👤 Individual Level

Personality, perception, motivation, learning, attitudes, emotions.

👥 Group Level

Team dynamics, leadership, communication, group decision-making.

🏢 Organizational Level

Structure, culture, change, climate, politics, conflict.

1.5 Organizational Climate

Definition

"Organizational Climate is the overall atmosphere or 'feel' of a workplace, shaped by its policies, leadership, and culture."

Think of it like the weather inside a company. Some companies feel warm, open, and encouraging. Others feel cold, strict, and stressful. That "feeling" is the organizational climate.

Factors That Shape Organizational Climate

  • Leadership style of managers
  • Communication patterns (open vs. closed)
  • Rules and policies of the organization
  • How conflicts are resolved
  • Degree of trust among employees
  • Rewards and recognition practices

Organizational Structure

Structure refers to how an organization is organized — who reports to whom, how tasks are divided, and how information flows.

  • Tall Structure: Many levels of management. Strict control. Common in large companies like the army.
  • Flat Structure: Few levels. More freedom for employees. Common in startups.
  • Matrix Structure: Employees report to multiple managers. Common in project-based companies.

Structure directly affects how people behave. A flat structure encourages creativity and quick decisions, while a tall structure creates order but can slow things down.

Individual Behaviour

2.1 Personality

Definition

"Personality is the sum total of ways in which an individual reacts to and interacts with others — a dynamic organization of characteristics."

In simple words: your personality is what makes you you. It is the unique pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviours that you consistently show over time.

Factors Influencing Personality

  • Heredity (Nature): Genes you are born with — physical appearance, energy level, temperament.
  • Environment (Nurture): Family, culture, friends, school, life experiences.
  • Situation: The same person behaves differently in a job interview vs. with close friends.

2.2 Learning

Definition

"Learning is any relatively permanent change in behaviour that occurs as a result of experience or practice."

Notice two key parts: permanent change (not just temporary) and experience/practice (not just reading a book).

Theories of Learning

  • Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Learning through association. Pavlov rang a bell every time he fed a dog. Eventually, the dog salivated just at the sound of the bell. In organizations: employees associate a good manager with a good work environment.
  • Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Learning through rewards and punishments.
    • Positive Reinforcement: Give reward → behaviour increases (e.g., bonus for meeting targets).
    • Negative Reinforcement: Remove punishment → behaviour increases (e.g., remove extra duties after improvement).
    • Punishment: Unpleasant consequence → behaviour decreases (e.g., warning letter).
    • Extinction: Remove reward → behaviour decreases (e.g., stop praising an employee who becomes lazy).
  • Social Learning (Bandura): Learning by watching others. Employees learn from their colleagues and role models in the organization.

The Learning Process

Learning in organizations follows a cycle: Experience → Reflect → Think → Experiment → New Experience. This is called Kolb's Learning Cycle.

2.3 Perception

Definition

"Perception is the process by which individuals organize and interpret their sensory impressions in order to give meaning to their environment."

Two people can see the same thing and interpret it completely differently. That is the power of perception. It is not reality — it is YOUR reality.

Factors Influencing Perception

  • The Perceiver: Your own attitudes, motives, interests, experiences, and expectations.
  • The Target: Novelty, motion, sounds, size, background of what you are perceiving.
  • The Situation: Time, work setting, social setting.

Perceptual Errors (Very Important!)

  • Halo Effect: Judging a person entirely based on one good trait. E.g., "She's well-dressed, so she must be smart."
  • Stereotyping: Generalizing about a group. E.g., "All engineers are introverts."
  • Selective Perception: Only noticing what we want to see and ignoring the rest.
  • Projection: Attributing your own traits to others. E.g., an honest person trusts everyone.
  • Contrast Effect: Evaluating a person compared to someone else instead of objectively.
💡 Why This Matters

Managers make hiring, promotion, and performance decisions based on perception. Knowing about perceptual errors helps make fairer, more objective judgments.

2.4 Emotional Intelligence (EI)

Definition

"Emotional Intelligence is the ability to recognize, understand, manage, and effectively use our own emotions and the emotions of others."

IQ (Intelligence Quotient) tells you how smart you are. EI (Emotional Intelligence) tells you how emotionally mature and socially skilled you are. Research shows EI is a better predictor of workplace success than IQ.

5 Components of EI (by Daniel Goleman)

  • Self-Awareness: Knowing your own emotions and how they affect others.
  • Self-Regulation: Controlling your impulses. Thinking before acting.
  • Motivation: Being driven to achieve for the sake of achievement, not just money.
  • Empathy: Understanding others' emotions. Putting yourself in their shoes.
  • Social Skills: Managing relationships, influencing others, teamwork, and communication.
💡 Example

A manager with high EI notices a team member seems stressed. Instead of ignoring it, they check in, offer support, and adjust workload — improving both morale and productivity.

2.5 Attitudes

Definition

"An attitude is a learned predisposition to respond in a consistently favourable or unfavourable manner with respect to a given object, person, or event."

Attitudes have three components (ABC model):

  • A — Affective: The emotional component. "I feel..." (e.g., I feel good about my job.)
  • B — Behavioural: The action component. "I intend to..." (e.g., I plan to stay at this job.)
  • C — Cognitive: The belief component. "I think/believe..." (e.g., I believe this company is fair.)

Key Work Attitudes

  • Job Satisfaction: How happy an employee is with their job.
  • Organizational Commitment: How loyal an employee feels toward the organization.
  • Employee Engagement: How enthusiastic and involved an employee is in their work.

2.6 Behaviour Modification

Behaviour Modification (OB Mod) is the systematic application of reinforcement theory to improve employee behaviour in organizations.

Steps in OB Modification:

  1. Identify: Find the specific behaviour that needs to change. (e.g., employees coming late)
  2. Measure: Track how often the behaviour occurs currently.
  3. Analyze: Find out the cause — what triggers the behaviour?
  4. Intervene: Use reinforcement (reward good behaviour, punish or ignore bad behaviour).
  5. Evaluate: Check if the behaviour has changed. Continue or adjust the strategy.

Unit Three · Groups
Group Behaviour

3.1 Understanding Groups

Definition

"A group is two or more individuals, interacting and interdependent, who have come together to achieve particular objectives."

Types of Groups

  • Formal Groups: Created by the organization for a specific purpose. E.g., project team, department.
  • Informal Groups: Formed naturally by employees based on friendship, common interests. E.g., lunch group, cricket fans at office.
  • Command Group: A manager and their direct reports.
  • Task Group: People working together to complete a specific task.
  • Interest Group: People with a common goal or concern.
  • Friendship Group: People who like each other and socialize.

Why Do People Join Groups?

  • Security — feeling less vulnerable alone
  • Status — prestige from belonging to a high-status group
  • Self-esteem — feeling valued and recognized
  • Affiliation — need for social interaction
  • Power — strength in numbers
  • Goal achievement — tasks that require more than one person

3.2 Stages of Group Development (Tuckman's Model)

Every group goes through five stages before it becomes fully effective:

1. Forming

Members are polite, cautious, and getting to know each other. Everyone is on best behaviour.

2. Storming

Conflicts arise! Disagreements about roles, ideas, and leadership. Tension is high.

3. Norming

Conflicts are resolved. Norms are set. Trust builds. The group starts to cooperate.

4. Performing

The group is fully functional. High energy, high productivity. Everyone knows their role.

5. Adjourning

The group disbands after completing its task. Members reflect and say goodbye.

3.3 Team Building

A team is a special kind of group where members have complementary skills, a shared purpose, and mutual accountability. All teams are groups, but not all groups are teams.

Characteristics of Effective Teams

  • Clear Goals: Everyone knows what they are working toward.
  • Complementary Skills: The team has a mix of technical, decision-making, and interpersonal skills.
  • Mutual Trust: Members rely on and support each other.
  • Open Communication: Ideas and feedback flow freely.
  • Shared Leadership: Leadership shifts based on the situation and expertise.
  • Constructive Conflict: Disagreements are handled professionally and improve decisions.

Team Building Activities

Organizations use activities like workshops, retreats, group problem-solving exercises, and role-play to build team cohesion and trust.

3.4 Communication in Organizations

Communication is the lifeblood of any organization. Without it, coordination is impossible.

Types of Communication

  • Verbal: Face-to-face, phone calls, meetings.
  • Written: Emails, reports, memos, letters.
  • Non-Verbal: Body language, facial expressions, tone of voice.

Direction of Communication

  • Downward: From managers to employees. E.g., instructions, policies.
  • Upward: From employees to managers. E.g., feedback, suggestions.
  • Lateral/Horizontal: Between peers at the same level.
  • Diagonal: Across different levels and departments.

Informal Communication — The Grapevine

The grapevine is the unofficial communication network in an organization. It is fast but often inaccurate. Managers must monitor the grapevine and address rumours quickly.

Barriers to Communication

  • Language differences
  • Noise and distractions
  • Emotional state (anger, fear)
  • Information overload
  • Perceptual differences
  • Status differences between sender and receiver

3.5 Group Decision Making

Group decisions are generally better than individual decisions because of diverse perspectives. But they can also face problems:

Advantages of Group Decisions

  • More information and knowledge available
  • Generates more alternatives
  • Higher acceptance by members (since they were involved)
  • Better quality decisions

Disadvantages (Group Decision Traps)

  • Groupthink: Desire for harmony overrides critical thinking. Everyone agrees even when they shouldn't. (Famous example: Challenger disaster)
  • Group Shift: Groups tend to make more extreme decisions than individuals — either more risky or more cautious.
  • Time-consuming
  • Dominant members can suppress others
💡 Preventing Groupthink

Assign a "devil's advocate" who always challenges the group's consensus. This forces critical thinking and prevents blind agreement.

Unit Four · Leadership
Leadership and Power

4.1 What is Leadership?

Definition

"Leadership is the ability to influence a group toward the achievement of a vision or set of goals."

A leader is someone who inspires others to follow — not because they have to, but because they want to. Leadership is about influence, not authority.

Importance of Leadership

  • Provides direction and vision to the team
  • Motivates employees during difficult times
  • Resolves conflicts and maintains team harmony
  • Helps organizations adapt to change
  • Builds trust and loyalty among employees

4.2 Leaders vs Managers — Key Difference

💡 Best of Both Worlds

The most effective people are both good leaders and good managers. You need vision AND the ability to execute it.

4.3 Leadership Styles

1. Autocratic (Authoritarian) Style

The leader makes all decisions alone. Employees have no say. Works well in emergencies or with unskilled workers. But kills creativity and motivation in the long run.

2. Democratic (Participative) Style

The leader involves the team in decisions. Boosts morale and creativity. Works well with skilled, motivated employees.

3. Laissez-Faire (Free-Rein) Style

The leader gives complete freedom to employees to make their own decisions. Works well with highly expert and self-motivated people (like research scientists). Can lead to chaos if the team lacks discipline.

4. Transformational Leadership

The leader inspires change by sharing a powerful vision and motivating followers to go beyond their own self-interest for the good of the group. E.g., Nelson Mandela, Steve Jobs.

5. Transactional Leadership

Based on exchange — "I will reward you if you do this." Focuses on maintaining performance standards through rewards and punishments.

6. Servant Leadership

The leader's primary role is to serve the team — removing obstacles, supporting growth, and putting employees first.

4.4 Theories of Leadership

1. Trait Theory

Leaders are born, not made. Certain traits (intelligence, confidence, integrity, charisma) make a person a leader. Limitation: Many good leaders don't have all these traits.

2. Behavioural Theories

What leaders do matters more than what they are. Two key dimensions: Task-Oriented (focus on getting work done) and People-Oriented (focus on employee welfare). The best leaders balance both. (Ohio State and Michigan Studies)

3. Contingency Theories

There is no single best leadership style. The best style depends on the situation.

  • Fiedler's Contingency Model: Match the leader to the situation. Task-oriented leaders excel in very favourable or unfavourable situations. People-oriented leaders excel in moderate situations.
  • Situational Leadership (Hersey & Blanchard): Adjust your style based on follower maturity (ability + willingness). Telling → Selling → Participating → Delegating.
  • Path-Goal Theory: Leaders motivate followers by clarifying the path to their goals and removing obstacles.

4.5 Power — Sources and Types

Definition

"Power is the capacity that A has to influence the behaviour of B, so that B acts in accordance with A's wishes."

French & Raven's 5 Bases of Power

1. Coercive Power

Power based on fear. "Do it or face punishment." E.g., threats of firing, demotion.

2. Reward Power

Power based on the ability to give rewards. E.g., bonuses, promotions, praise.

3. Legitimate Power

Power from your formal position or title. E.g., a CEO has legitimate power over employees.

4. Expert Power

Power from knowledge or skills. E.g., the IT expert everyone relies on.

5. Referent Power

Power from admiration and respect. E.g., a charismatic leader people follow out of love.

Power vs Authority

Authority is formal (given by the organization). Power can be informal (earned through expertise, personality, or fear). A person can have power without authority and vice versa.

Organizational Politics

Organizational politics involves actions taken to acquire, develop, and use power and other resources to obtain preferred outcomes in situations of uncertainty. It is inevitable — the key is managing it ethically.

Unit Five · Change & Culture
Dynamics of Organizational Behaviour

5.1 Organizational Culture

Definition

"Organizational culture is the system of shared beliefs, values, and norms that distinguish one organization from another and guide employee behaviour."

Culture is "how we do things around here." It is invisible but powerfully shapes every decision, behaviour, and interaction in an organization.

Characteristics of Organizational Culture

  • Innovation & Risk Taking: Do employees feel encouraged to experiment?
  • Attention to Detail: How precise and analytical are people expected to be?
  • Outcome Orientation: Is focus on results or process?
  • People Orientation: How much does management consider employee impact?
  • Team Orientation: Is work organized around individuals or teams?
  • Aggressiveness: Are people competitive or easy-going?
  • Stability: Does the organization prefer maintaining the status quo or growth?

Strong vs Weak Culture

  • Strong Culture: Core values are widely shared and deeply held. Employees behave consistently. E.g., Google, Apple.
  • Weak Culture: Values are not clearly defined. Employees are unsure how to behave.
💡 Culture Eats Strategy for Breakfast

Even the best strategy will fail if the organizational culture doesn't support it. Culture is the foundation of everything.

5.2 Job Satisfaction

Definition

"Job satisfaction is a positive feeling about one's job resulting from an evaluation of its characteristics."

It is simply: How much do you like your job?

Factors Influencing Job Satisfaction

  • The work itself (interesting and meaningful work)
  • Pay and benefits
  • Promotion opportunities
  • Quality of supervision
  • Relationships with coworkers
  • Working conditions

Consequences of Job Satisfaction/Dissatisfaction

Satisfied EmployeesDissatisfied Employees

Stay in the organization (loyalty)Higher turnover (leave the company)
Better customer servicePoor customer interactions

5.3 Organizational Change

Definition

"Organizational change is the process by which organizations move from their present state to some desired future state to increase their effectiveness."

Change is constant in the modern world. Technology changes, markets change, governments change laws. Organizations must change to survive.

Types of Change

  • Planned Change: Deliberate and intentional. E.g., implementing a new software system.
  • Unplanned Change: Forced by external events. E.g., COVID-19 forcing remote work.
  • Incremental Change: Small, gradual improvements. E.g., improving a process step by step.
  • Radical/Transformational Change: Major overhaul of the entire organization. E.g., a company shifting from manufacturing to software.

Lewin's 3-Step Change Model

Step 1: Unfreeze

Break old habits and mindsets. Make people understand WHY change is needed.

Step 2: Change (Move)

Implement the actual change. Provide training, support, and resources.

Step 3: Refreeze

Stabilize the change. Make the new way the standard. Reinforce and reward new behaviours.

5.4 Resistance to Change

Almost everyone resists change to some degree. This is natural — change creates uncertainty and threatens what we know.

Individual Sources of Resistance

  • Habit: It is easier to keep doing things the familiar way.
  • Security: Change threatens job security and comfort.
  • Fear of the Unknown: People fear what they cannot predict.
  • Selective Information Processing: People ignore information that challenges their views.
  • Economic Factors: Fear of losing income.

Organizational Sources of Resistance

  • Structural inertia (existing systems resist change)
  • Limited focus of change (change in one part upsets another)
  • Group norms (the group's way of doing things)
  • Threat to power relationships

Overcoming Resistance to Change

  • Education & Communication: Explain WHY the change is needed.
  • Participation: Involve employees in planning the change.
  • Support & Facilitation: Provide training and emotional support.
  • Negotiation: Offer incentives to those who resist.
  • Coercion (Last Resort): Mandate the change when necessary.

5.5 Work Stress

Definition

"Stress is a dynamic condition in which an individual is confronted with an opportunity, constraint, or demand related to what they desire and the outcome is perceived to be both uncertain and important."

Simply: Stress is the feeling that demands placed on you exceed your ability to cope.

Sources of Work Stress (Stressors)

  • Task Demands: Too much work (overload), tight deadlines, unclear job responsibilities.
  • Role Demands: Role ambiguity (unclear expectations), role conflict (conflicting demands).
  • Interpersonal Demands: Difficult coworkers, poor leadership, bullying.
  • Organizational Structure: Too many rules, excessive monitoring, no employee involvement.
  • Work-Life Balance: Long hours leaving no personal time.

Effects of Stress

  • Physical: Headaches, heart disease, sleep disorders, low immunity.
  • Psychological: Anxiety, depression, burnout, irritability.
  • Behavioural: Absenteeism, low productivity, substance abuse, aggressive behaviour.

Managing Stress — Individual Strategies

  • Time management — prioritize and organize your work.
  • Exercise regularly — physical activity reduces cortisol (the stress hormone).
  • Relaxation techniques — meditation, deep breathing, yoga.
  • Build a social support network — talk to friends and family.
  • Seek professional help if stress becomes overwhelming.

Managing Stress — Organizational Strategies

  • Improved personnel selection — match people to the right roles.
  • Realistic job previews — don't oversell the role during hiring.
  • Goal setting — clear, achievable targets reduce ambiguity.
  • Employee assistance programs (EAPs) — counselling services.
  • Flexible work arrangements — remote work, flextime.
  • Wellness programs — gym, mental health days, healthy cafeteria.
💡 Good Stress vs Bad Stress

Eustress is positive stress — the excitement before a presentation, the buzz of a challenging project. It improves performance. Distress is negative stress that overwhelms and damages health. The goal is not zero stress — it is the right amount.

5.6 Work-Life Balance and Organizational Effectiveness

Organizations that care about work-life balance see measurable results:

  • Higher employee satisfaction and morale
  • Lower absenteeism and turnover
  • Greater creativity (a rested mind is a creative mind)
  • Better physical and mental health outcomes
  • Stronger employer brand — attracts top talent

The modern organization understands that employees are not machines. Sustainable performance requires rest, recovery, and purpose. An organization's long-term success is deeply tied to the wellbeing of the humans who power it.
